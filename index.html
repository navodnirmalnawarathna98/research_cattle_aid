Cattle AID

Application of Ontology-Based Veterinary Information Extraction for Cattle Skin Disease Diagnosis

Elevating Cattle Wellness

In the dynamic landscape of veterinary medicine, technological innovations have emerged as crucial tools for addressing the challenges faced by livestock industries. In this context, our research endeavours to revolutionize the diagnosis and management of cattle skin diseases through the integration of advanced technologies and domain-specific knowledge. Our research topic, "Application of Ontology-Based Veterinary Information Extraction for Cattle Skin Disease Diagnosis, " encapsulates a multidimensional approach that harnesses the power of artificial intelligence (AI) and ontology-based information extraction to enhance the understanding and handling of cattle skin diseases.

LITERATURE SURVEY

Skin illnesses in cattle significantly affect animal welfare and agricultural productivity, posing challenges for veterinarians and farmers (Radostits et al., 2007). Traditional diagnostic techniques often rely on manual evaluation, leading to errors and delays (Bonfini et al., 2018). Advances in technology, such as image analysis algorithms using machine learning, offer promising solutions for automatic detection and classification of skin disorders (Al-Tamimi et al., 2020; Bromley et al., 2018). Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), popularized by LeCun et al. (1998) and Krizhevsky et al. (2012), have revolutionized image processing tasks. Additionally, word embeddings like Word2Vec and GloVe (Mikolov et al., 2013; Pennington et al., 2014) enhance natural language processing by capturing semantic relationships between words. Transformer-based models like BERT and GPT have further advanced language understanding (Devlin et al., 2018; Radford et al., 2018). Semantic web technologies, such as RDF, OWL, and SPARQL, improve data interoperability and knowledge representation. Integrating veterinary expertise into diagnostic algorithms can enhance the accuracy and reliability of automated systems (Valente et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2024).

METHODOLOGY


Farmers must register and log in to a system that allows them to report livestock issues, particularly skin diseases. Upon logging in, they can describe symptoms and provide relevant details. The system employs advanced technologies, including image recognition and ontology web technologies, to diagnose conditions, assess severity, and retrieve treatment information from a knowledge base. Veterinarians review and verify the treatment data, ensuring accuracy. The system keeps detailed health records for intelligent analysis and trend identification, enhancing decision-making for farmers. Security and privacy are prioritized, and reinforcement learning updates treatment plans based on veterinary feedback. This comprehensive system provides timely diagnoses, fosters communication with veterinarians, and supports farmers in managing cattle health effectively.

RESEARCH PROBLEM

Due to their reliance on human evaluation, the current methods for detecting and treating skin problems in cattle have limitations that cause delays and mistakes in the process. Furthermore, these approaches frequently lack integration and don't take into account the psychological needs of users who are coping with the difficulties of managing cattle health, such as farmers and veterinarians. Consequently, there is an urgent need for an integrated diagnostic and support platform that makes use of state-of-the-art technologies, such as sentiment analysis and image analysis algorithms, to improve the identification, diagnosis, and treatment of skin diseases in cattle while offering users individualized emotional support.

RESEARCH GAP

The cattle industry in Sri Lanka, a vital component of the agricultural sector, is pivotal to the nation's economy. However, the occurrence of skin diseases among cattle remains a pressing concern, impacting animal welfare, productivity, and economic stability. The conventional methods of diagnosing these diseases often rely on subjective visual assessments, leading to inconsistencies and delays in treatment. Moreover, the accurate assessment of disease severity and the efficient extraction of relevant information from diverse data sources pose formidable challenges.
