Cattle AID

Application of Ontology-Based Veterinary Information Extraction for Cattle Skin Disease Diagnosis

Elevating Cattle Wellness

In the dynamic landscape of veterinary medicine, technological innovations have emerged as crucial tools for addressing the challenges faced by livestock industries. In this context, our research endeavours to revolutionize the diagnosis and management of cattle skin diseases through the integration of advanced technologies and domain-specific knowledge. Our research topic, "Application of Ontology-Based Veterinary Information Extraction for Cattle Skin Disease Diagnosis, " encapsulates a multidimensional approach that harnesses the power of artificial intelligence (AI) and ontology-based information extraction to enhance the understanding and handling of cattle skin diseases.

LITERATURE SURVEY

Animal welfare and agricultural output are negatively impacted by skin illnesses in cattle, which provide serious difficulties to veterinarians and farmers globally (Radostits et al., 2007). According to Bonfini et al. (2018), [1] traditional diagnostic techniques frequently rely on manual evaluation, which causes errors and delays in the identification and treatment of diseases. This calls for the creation of novel strategies to improve cow skin disease diagnostics and treatment. Promising solutions to these problems can be found in recent technological breakthroughs. Al-Tamimi et al. (2020) have noted that the field of veterinary medicine has been using image analysis algorithms more and more to automatically detect and classify skin disorders. According to Bromley et al. (2018),[2] these algorithms use machine learning approaches to promptly and accurately identify patients based on their visual analysis. Veterinarians may quickly diagnose cattle skin problems by incorporating these algorithms into diagnostic platforms. This allows for prompt intervention and treatment (Velroyen et al., 2019). Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have revolutionized image processing jobs, especially in the areas of segmentation, classification, and object detection. LeCun et al. (1998) and Krizhevsky et al. (2012) popularized CNNs [4], which completely changed the discipline by offering architectures that could learn hierarchical representations from raw pixel data. With the help of this feature, CNNs can recognize objects in photos, segment them to determine their borders and classify them into predetermined groups. A testament to CNNs' effectiveness at these tasks is the vast range of applications they have been used in, from autonomous driving to medical imaging. Notably, CNN-based methods have shown their adaptability and effectiveness in a variety of image-processing tasks by achieving state-of-the-art performance in benchmark datasets and real-world settings. Word embeddings, like those developed by Mikolov et al. (2013) and Pennington et al. (2014)'s GloVe, are essential for comprehending natural language because they capture the semantic connections between words. With these methods, words are depicted as dense vectors in a continuous vector space, with comparable words grouped together. By utilizing the distributional hypothesis, Word2Vec assumes that words that appear in comparable settings typically have comparable meanings. Similarly, GloVe[5] learns word representations that capture both local and global word co-occurrence statistics by fusing global matrix factorization with local context window approaches. These methods enable models to capture semantic similarities between words and phrases, which helps with a variety of natural language processing (NLP) tasks like sentiment analysis, named entity recognition, and machine translation. They do this by embedding words into continuous vector spaces. Moreover, by utilizing self-attention processes to more efficiently capture contextual information, transformer-based models like BERT (Devlin et al., 2018)[6],[7],[8] and GPT (Radford et al., 2018) have completely changed natural language processing (NLP). Using huge text corpora, BERT, for example, pre-trains a bidirectional transformer model to obtain state-of-the-art performance across many NLP benchmarks and capture bidirectional context. In a similar vein, GPT generates text that is both coherent and contextually relevant by training a transformer decoder architecture on a variety of text sources. Because these transformer-based models develop rich contextual representations of words and phrases, they have considerably advanced language understanding tasks. Semantic web technologies offer a defined framework for formal, machine-understandable ontology representation and querying, such as RDF (Resource Description Framework) and OWL (Web Ontology Language). RDF provides the basis for representing data as triples (subject, predicate, and object), which enables the construction of connected data graphs. In contrast, OWL builds upon RDF by offering a more comprehensive vocabulary and formal semantics for constructing ontologies, which include persons, classes, and attributes, as well as axioms for establishing relationships and logical constraints between entities. These technologies facilitate the integration and interoperability of disparate data sources by enabling the creation of knowledge graphs that encapsulate intricate linkages and domain-specific information. Furthermore, SPARQL (SPARQL Protocol and RDF Query Language),[9],[10] one of the potent querying techniques that RDF and OWL allow, enables users to obtain and manipulate data from ontologies flexibly and expressively. Organizations can improve their data interoperability, knowledge representation, and reasoning skills by utilizing semantic web technologies. This can lead to more efficient information management and decision-making procedures. Moreover, veterinary knowledge must be incorporated to guarantee the accuracy and dependability of automated diagnostic systems. Veterinarians can improve the accuracy of disease identification and severity evaluation since they have specific knowledge and expertise (Valente et al., 2021).[3] Researchers can create more reliable systems that can provide thorough and customized treatment suggestions by incorporating veterinary expertise into diagnostic algorithms (Lee et al., 2024).

METHODOLOGY


Farmers must log in to the system in order to use its features. Farmers must first register with the system. During the registration process, you will be asked to enter your name, contact information, farm location, and any other pertinent information. Farmers can report problems with their livestock, especially skin illnesses, after logging in. They are able to explain the signs and provide any further pertinent details on the problem they are having with their animals. The system uses cutting-edge technologies to automatically detect the condition and determine its severity level when it receives a report of a skin disease issue. This could entail using additional diagnostic techniques or image recognition technologies to analyze pictures of the afflicted cattle's skin. The system first detects the skin condition and then uses ontology web technologies to retrieve therapeutic information from its knowledge base. Ontologies are used to structure and make sense of knowledge concerning diseases that affect cattle, their symptoms, and the therapies that are associated with them. Following the extraction of the treatment data, the system uses the application to start a conversation with a veterinarian. The extracted treatment data are reviewed by the veterinarian to ensure that they are correct and suitable for the detected skin illness and its severity level. The system's capacity to keep thorough records makes it possible to analyze trends and patterns in the health of cattle in an intelligent manner. By providing farmers with insightful data, this data-driven strategy enables them to make well-informed decisions regarding the management of their animals. Furthermore, farmers feel more comfortable providing critical information with the system because of its dedication to security and privacy. The technology uses reinforcement learning techniques to update the therapy details when the veterinarian verifies them. This can entail updating and optimizing the treatment plan by incorporating any fresh information from the veterinarian. The system under consideration offers a comprehensive approach to the management of skin diseases in cattle kept in agricultural environments. In order to guarantee farmers' privacy and accountability, the system starts with a secure login procedure. Farmers can report skin disease issues affecting their cattle after registering. With the use of cutting-edge technologies, the system provides farmers with a timely and precise diagnosis by automatically detecting the disease and evaluating its severity. Subsequently, ontology web technologies retrieve comprehensive therapy data specific to the diagnosed ailment. The system makes communication with a veterinarian via the application to verify the effectiveness of the treatment. In the course of treatment, this cooperative approach cultivates trust and knowledge. Additionally, the system uses reinforcement learning techniques to update treatment specifics on a regular basis depending on real-world results and veterinary comments. Farmers feel more confident in managing diseases and treatments work better because to this iterative method. The system acknowledges the emotional impact that livestock health issues have on farmers in addition to its technical features. The program seeks to reduce stress and foster a sense of empowerment among farmers by offering prompt assistance and knowledgeable direction. All things considered, the integrated system provides an intuitive, compassionate, and successful method of treating skin disease issues in cattle in agricultural settings.

RESEARCH PROBLEM

Due to their reliance on human evaluation, the current methods for detecting and treating skin problems in cattle have limitations that cause delays and mistakes in the process. Furthermore, these approaches frequently lack integration and don't take into account the psychological needs of users who are coping with the difficulties of managing cattle health, such as farmers and veterinarians. Consequently, there is an urgent need for an integrated diagnostic and support platform that makes use of state-of-the-art technologies, such as sentiment analysis and image analysis algorithms, to improve the identification, diagnosis, and treatment of skin diseases in cattle while offering users individualized emotional support.

RESEARCH GAP

The cattle industry in Sri Lanka, a vital component of the agricultural sector, is pivotal to the nation's economy. However, the occurrence of skin diseases among cattle remains a pressing concern, impacting animal welfare, productivity, and economic stability. The conventional methods of diagnosing these diseases often rely on subjective visual assessments, leading to inconsistencies and delays in treatment. Moreover, the accurate assessment of disease severity and the efficient extraction of relevant information from diverse data sources pose formidable challenges.
